Projects

Contextually aware social matching

Social matching systems aim at supporting the
creation of new social ties by recommending people
to people. In theory, such systems could decrease
social barriers for initiating face-to-face interaction
with an unfamiliar person and increase social capital.
However, social matching systems currently do not
have effective mechanisms to mediate chance
encounters and introduce relevant people to each
other at the opportune moment and the opportune
place in order to encourage face-to-face interaction.
Chance encounters rely on opportunity, meaning that
two people have to be in a situation where their paths
intersect and they have the opportunity to interact.

Online dating

Social matching systems have made it possible to
meet people in the physical world in ways that were
formerly impossible due to geographical, temporal,
and social limitations. A key need for users is the
ability to form impressions of other people online
that they deem accurate once validated face-to-face.
However, we lack a comprehensive understanding of
how users evaluate information about each other
within these systems for face-to-face meetings.
Online dating systems are perhaps the most popular
form of social matching systems today, yet research
suggests online daters are plagued with impression
formation struggles [2, 4].  Research conducted at the
Connections Lab uses online dating as a context to
understand people’s abilities to form accurate
impressions of strangers online for face-to-face
meetings, and how such abilities can be better
facilitated.

The Team

Faculty

Dr.Quentin Jones, Associate Professor

Dr. Quentin Jones is an Associate Professor with a Ph.D. in
Information Systems from University of Haifa, Israel.  He has
received many grants from National Science Foundation to
develop user-location and social-activity aware mobile
applications for community building, collaboration and customer
discovery.  To achieve that goal, Dr. Jones built an NJIT test-bed
with NSF funding – a wireless and mobile environment for
location-based online community. He currently supervises four
Ph.D. students working in the area of human-centered
computing. Their work advances understanding of connections
through social computing applications, specifically: (1) social
activity coordination; (2) social activity group coalescing; (3)
context-aware social matching; and (4) impression management
in online dating systems.
Website: http://is.njit.edu/people/jones.php
