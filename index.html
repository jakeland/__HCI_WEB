About

Our lab studies how people build - and maintain - social ties through technology. As mobile social applications become an essential part of our social fabric, they are transforming the way we make new social ties and redefining human connection and communication.

Our goal is to transform the experience people have with new technologies. From understanding user needs, to developing and evaluating those technologies, the lab's faculty, staff, and students have been leading the way in HCI research and teaching. We use qualitative and quantitative methods as well as research- through-design approaches.

Our work lies at the intersection of Human-Computer-Interaction (HCI), Computer-Supported Cooperative Work (CSCW) and Ubiquitous Computing.

Projects

Context-aware Social Matching

Social matching systems aim at supporting the creation of new social ties by recommending people to people. In theory, such systems could decrease social barriers for initiating face-to-face interaction with an unfamiliar person and increase social capital. However, social matching systems currently do not have effective mechanisms to mediate chance encounters and introduce relevant people to each other at the opportune moment and the opportune place in order to encourage face-to-face interaction. Chance encounters rely on opportunity, meaning that two people have to be in a situation where their paths intersect and they have the opportunity to interact.

(Supported by the Natural Science Foundation)

Social Activity Coordination

One of the primary uses of interpersonal communication channels such as texting, instant messaging and mobile phone conversations is to coordinate social activities, such as the planning of a future face-to-face meeting. The goal of this project is to lay the foundation, through the instantiation of innovative software that embodies lessons learned from studies into everyday social coordination practices, for future work that will significantly expand our knowledge of how to design mobile outeraction-support systems. A particular focus will be to address people's desire for a shared view of the status of the activity planning process (e.g., knowing who can come to an event at a particular time).

(Supported by the Natural Science Foundation)

Group Coalescing

Despite the importance of individuals coming together for social group activities, the process by which such groups coalesce is poorly understood. Because of this, current technologies that have been built for the purpose of bringing people together have not addressed the social burdens that exist in organizing activities. The goal of this research is to understand the group coalescing process and the extent to which it is suboptimal in urban communities. To address this problem, we will design technological solutions that reduce the burdens individuals face in becoming an organizer. Reducing the burden of leadership effectively increases the chance an individual will step forward to begin the coalescing of a social activity group, leading to successful group activities.

Online Dating

Social matching systems have made it possible to meet people in the physical world in ways that were formerly impossible due to geographical, temporal, and social limitations. A key need for users is the ability to form impressions of other people online that they deem accurate once validated face-to-face. However, we lack a comprehensive understanding of how users evaluate information about each other within these systems for face-to-face meetings. Online dating systems are perhaps the most popular form of social matching systems today, yet research suggests online daters are plagued with impression formation struggles [2, 4]. Research conducted at the Connections Lab uses online dating as a context to understand peopleâ€™s abilities to form accurate impressions of strangers online for face-to-face meetings, and how such abilities can be better facilitated.
